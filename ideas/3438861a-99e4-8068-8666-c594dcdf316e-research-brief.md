## Problem summary

Homeowners accumulate a backlog of improvement and maintenance projects but lack a tool that treats them as a true prioritized queue rather than a passive list. The friction points are threefold: storing project-specific context (measurements, materials, shopping lists) in a single place, knowing what to tackle next given budget and season, and generating a realistic action plan. Most people fall back on a Notes app or a physical list, losing context and never making progress on the backlog systematically.

## Market landscape

The home management app space is crowded but segmented into two camps that don't overlap well:

**Maintenance reminder apps** (HomeKeep, Dwellin, HomeManager) — focused on recurring seasonal tasks (change HVAC filters, clean gutters). Reactive, checklist-oriented, no project context storage.

**Home management suites** (HomeZada) — broader scope including inventory, budgeting, project tracking, and document storage. Closest to what House Hopper is proposing, but the UX is dense and the AI layer (ROI-based project suggestions) is shallow. No queue mental model and no shopping list generation tied to project plans.

**DIY project planners** (MOREPHO, DIY Project Planner) — simple task/checklist apps with photo and budget tracking. No AI prioritization, no seasonal awareness.

**Contractor marketplaces** (Angi, Thumbtack, TaskRabbit, Houzz Pro) — focused on connecting homeowners with pros, not on personal project planning or tracking.

**General AI task managers** (Motion, Reclaim, Trevor AI) — strong prioritization and scheduling but not domain-specific. No concept of home project context, measurements, or seasonal constraints.

The gap: no app combines a queue-first mental model + project-scoped context storage (measurements, shopping lists) + AI prioritization across difficulty, season, and cost simultaneously.

## Prior art + competitors

| App | Strengths | Weaknesses vs. House Hopper |
|---|---|---|
| HomeZada | Most complete home suite; project tracking, inventory, budget, some AI | Dense UX; AI is ROI-focused not queue-prioritization; no shopping list generation |
| MOREPHO | Simple project-first UI; photo + budget per project | No AI; no measurements; no prioritization |
| Nines | Task management with household member assignment, recurring tasks | Not project-scoped; no measurements or shopping lists; no AI |
| Dwellin | Seasonal reminders, recurring chore automation | Maintenance only; not home improvement projects |
| HomeZada AI | ROI analysis on improvement projects | Narrow AI use case; not a full planning assistant |
| Notion/spreadsheets | Fully flexible; power users use these | No AI, no domain-specific context, high setup cost |

No existing product delivers: a ranked project queue + AI-generated action plan + integrated shopping list + measurement storage + prioritization by difficulty/season/cost.

## Open questions

1. **DIY vs. hire-out scope** — Does the AI plan assume the user is doing the work themselves, or does it also help them scope work for contractors? This changes the output format significantly.
2. **What drives AI prioritization?** — Does it use a weather/location API for seasonality, or is season user-defined? Is difficulty self-reported or inferred from project type?
3. **Shopping list depth** — Is the list a free-text scratchpad or structured (item, quantity, store, price)? Integration with Home Depot/Lowe's would be valuable but adds scope.
4. **Measurement model** — Are measurements freeform notes or structured fields (length, area, volume)? Structured data enables smarter material quantity suggestions.
5. **Project dependencies** — How does the queue handle projects with hard dependencies (e.g., must finish drywall before painting)?
6. **Budget awareness** — Is there a per-project budget or a total available budget that the AI uses to sort the queue?

## Recommendation

**Pursue.** The gap is real and no existing product fills it cleanly. The core insight — treat home projects as a priority queue, not a passive list, with AI helping you decide what to do next — is differentiated and solves a genuine daily frustration for homeowners.

**Recommended scope for v1:** Queue-based project cards with freeform measurement notes and a simple shopping list per project. AI prioritization based on three user-defined inputs: rough cost range, physical difficulty (easy/medium/hard), and ideal season. No contractor integration in v1. Avoid structured measurements or store API integration until the core queue loop is validated.

**Key risk:** HomeZada is already broad-scope and could add a queue feature. The moat is UX simplicity — House Hopper should feel like a queue management tool, not a home management suite. Resist feature creep toward inventory and document storage in early versions.
